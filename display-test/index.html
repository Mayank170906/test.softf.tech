How to Get the Best Results

Follow these simple steps to perform a thorough and accurate check of your monitor.

  • 1. Prepare Your Environment

    For best results, test in a dimly lit room to make issues like backlight bleed more obvious. Make sure your monitor is clean and free of dust or smudges.

  • 2. Use Fullscreen Mode

    Our tests are designed to run in fullscreen. This hides distractions and allows you to inspect the very edges of your display for problems.

  • 3. Know What to Look For

    In the solid color tests, look for tiny dots that are the wrong color (dead or stuck pixels). In the uniformity test, look for bright patches, especially near the edges (backlight bleed).

Online Monitor Test Guide

This guide explains what the display test does, how it works, and when you should use it to verify the condition of your screen.

What This Online Monitor Test Is

This page is a browser-based monitor testing utility that checks the physical quality of your display. It shows fullscreen patterns and motion elements designed to reveal hardware defects that everyday applications often hide.

The test runs entirely inside your web browser, so you do not need to download or install any software. No system information is collected and nothing is modified on your device.

How the Display Test Works

The tool displays controlled colors, gradients, motion objects and contrast patterns across the full screen. These patterns force every pixel to change state so you can visually inspect the panel.

If a monitor has a defect, it becomes visible during these patterns as dots, uneven lighting, blur, brightness patches or motion trails that are difficult to notice during normal use.

Why You Should Test Your Screen

Many displays ship with minor panel defects that users only notice after the replacement period ends. Running a screen test immediately after buying a monitor or laptop helps confirm the display is working properly while it is still under warranty.

The test is useful for gaming, office work, programming, and photo editing where clear text, accurate colors and smooth motion are important for comfortable viewing.

Supported Devices

The monitor test works on desktop monitors, gaming displays, laptops, ultrawide screens, televisions and tablets. It supports LCD, LED, IPS, VA and OLED panels across Windows, macOS, Linux and mobile browsers.

Because the page only renders visual patterns, it is safe to use and does not change any monitor or system settings.

Monitor Tests Explained

Each screen test is designed to detect a specific display problem. Use the descriptions below to understand what to look for while testing your monitor.

Dead Pixel Test (Solid Color Test)

Displays fullscreen red, green, blue, black and white screens to detect dead pixels, stuck pixels and hot pixels. A dead pixel appears as a permanent black dot while a stuck pixel shows a constant colored dot.

Gradient Test (Color Banding Test)

Shows smooth color transitions to identify color banding. Visible lines or steps instead of smooth gradients indicate poor panel quality or incorrect color depth settings.

Contrast Test

Checks black and white levels to ensure proper contrast ratio. Helps determine whether dark details are crushed or bright areas are overexposed.

Backlight Bleed & Screen Uniformity Test

Uses dark screens to reveal uneven brightness, clouding, and backlight bleed. Bright patches near edges are common in LCD and LED monitors.

Sharpness & Text Clarity Test

Displays text patterns to evaluate image sharpness and scaling. Helps verify resolution settings and detect blurry fonts caused by incorrect display configuration.

Viewing Angle Test

Shows color patterns to check color shifting when viewing the screen from different angles. Useful for comparing TN, VA and IPS panel performance.

Ghosting & Response Time Test

Displays moving objects to detect ghosting and motion blur. Trails behind moving shapes indicate slow pixel response time, common in older or low-quality panels.

Refresh Rate Checker

Measures the actual refresh rate of your display and confirms whether your monitor is running at 60Hz, 120Hz, 144Hz or higher. Many high-refresh monitors default to 60Hz unless manually enabled.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a dead pixel and a stuck pixel?

A **dead pixel** is a pixel that remains unlit (black) at all times. This is typically a hardware defect that cannot be fixed. A **stuck pixel** is a pixel that is permanently stuck on one color (usually red, green, or blue) because one of its sub-pixels is always on. Stuck pixels can sometimes be fixed with software tools.

How do I test for backlight bleed?

The best way to test for backlight bleed is to use the "Screen Uniformity" test. Select the black or dark gray color pattern, make your room as dark as possible, and increase your monitor's brightness. Look for patches of light "bleeding" in from the edges of the screen. This is most common on LCD/LED displays.

Is this test compatible with OLED displays?

Yes, all tests except the "Screen Uniformity" test for backlight bleed are fully compatible with OLED displays. Since OLED pixels produce their own light, they do not have a backlight and therefore cannot suffer from backlight bleed or clouding. The dead pixel and color tests are still very useful for OLED screens.

How do I use this online monitor test?

It's simple. Use the buttons to cycle through the different test patterns. We recommend going full-screen (press F11) in a dark room. Carefully inspect the screen during each color test (Red, Green, Blue, White, Black) to find any dead or stuck pixels. Use the other patterns to check for uniformity, ghosting, and color banding.

Can this tool fix a stuck pixel?

This tool is primarily for *testing* and *finding* pixels. However, some users have success fixing stuck pixels by rapidly cycling through different colors. You can try our "Stuck Pixel Fixer" tool, which flashes high-contrast colors to try and "unstick" the pixel. This is not guaranteed to work and has no effect on dead pixels.

What is screen ghosting or motion blur?

Ghosting (or motion blur) is a trail or "ghost" image left behind by moving objects on a screen. It's caused by a monitor's slow response time. Our "Ghosting Test" (often a UFO or moving box pattern) is designed to make this effect obvious. A high-refresh-rate monitor with a fast response time will show a much clearer moving image.

Is this monitor test tool safe and private?

Yes, 100%. This tool is a simple web page that runs entirely in your browser. It does not install any software, and no data about your monitor or system is sent to our servers. It's completely safe and private to use.
